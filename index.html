How it works

The technology combines acoustic and mechanical resonance to enlarge submicron aerosol particles. Ultrasonic emitters excite thin elastic resonator plates. A standing wave forms in the gaps between the plates, while the plates oscillate at their natural frequency, amplifying the acoustic field. Three coagulation mechanisms work simultaneously: PM2.5 particles gather at standing wave nodes, collide due to velocity gradients, and are captured by vibrations near the plate surfaces, forming agglomerates.

AIRSONIC Module

Acoustic coagulation system for cleanroom air purification.

  • •Ultrasonic resonators (20 kHz) enlarge PM2.5 particles through a standing wave field
  • •HEPA filter lifespan +50–100%, energy consumption −20–25%
  • •Modular design for flexible integration

Applications: Pharmaceutical cleanrooms (GMP A/B), semiconductors (ISO 5–7), medical facilities

Sonic Lock Technology

Independent particle blockage system through oscillating rods.

  • •Pre-tensioned steel rods capture PM2.5 by inertial interception
  • •6× increase in particle capture compared to stationary case
  • •Composition-independent, full blockage of target particle sizes

Application: Pre-treatment barrier upstream of HEPA filters

ABOUT THE PROJECT

What is RESPIRATION?

The World Health Organization has identified air pollution as one of the most serious environmental hazards to human health. Fine particles with a diameter of less than 2.5 microns (PM2.5) can penetrate deep into the lungs and even into the circulatory system, adversely affecting cardiovascular and respiratory systems.

The RESPIRATION project aims to solve this problem by increasing the efficiency of air purification technology using acoustic coagulation with resonators.

The pioneering nature of the project lies in using resonators located in the path of gas flow, combined with computer modeling by the finite element method to meaningfully consider all factors influencing acoustic coagulation.

For the General Public

The World Health Organization has identified the problem of air pollution as one of the most serious environmental hazards to human health at the level of climate change. Classic "pollutants" include fine particles, ozone, nitrogen dioxide, sulfur dioxide and carbon monoxide. Of particular concern are fine particles with a diameter of less than 10 and 2.5 microns (PM10 and PM2.5). PM2.5 can penetrate even into the circulatory system, adversely affecting the cardiovascular and respiratory systems and internal organs.

This problem is most common in middle- and low-income countries. For the first time since 2005, the WHO has revised PM10 and PM2.5 emission standards. Mortality from coronary heart disease accounts for 16% of deaths; chronic obstructive pulmonary disease 11% and stroke 6%.

Beyond direct health impacts, air pollution raises the cost of medicines and precision manufacturing (military, aerospace, pharmaceutical) performed in controlled premises.

Project Aims

The project aims to solve the problem of purification of air from fine particles (diameter < 2.5 μm) by increasing the efficiency of pre-treatment technology with an acoustic field using resonators.

Pioneering Nature

  • Use of resonators located in the path of the gas to be purified.
  • Finite element method computer modeling to consider all factors influencing acoustic coagulation.

Adding resonators: creates an additional barrier to airflow, complicates particle trajectory oscillations, and allows obtaining high acoustic pressure values at lower energy consumption through the resonance phenomenon.

Project Results

1. Computer and mathematical models for the design phase of air purification systems, scalable from laboratory to industrial installations.

2. Results to be implemented in a PhD dissertation.

3. Models introduced into the educational process at the National Technical University of Ukraine "Igor Sikorsky Kyiv Polytechnic Institute".

4. 2 articles in peer-reviewed journals (top 10 Scopus) and 2 Open Access publications planned.

5. Coverage at 3 international conferences.

6. Materials used to prepare a Horizon Europe application.

7. Increased research capacity of Łukasiewicz Research Network.
